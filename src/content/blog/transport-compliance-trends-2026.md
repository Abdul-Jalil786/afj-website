Compliance is the foundation of every reputable transport operation. Without it, vehicles cannot legally operate, contracts cannot be retained and, most importantly, the safety of passengers cannot be assured. As 2026 begins, several compliance trends are shaping how transport providers manage their obligations, from evolving regulatory requirements to new technologies that make compliance easier to track and demonstrate.

For operators delivering SEND transport, Non-Emergency Patient Transport Services and private hire, staying ahead of these trends is not optional. It is a competitive and operational necessity.

## Enhanced DBS and Workforce Vetting

The safeguarding landscape continues to evolve, with increasing expectations around the vetting and monitoring of staff who work with vulnerable passengers.

**DBS Update Service adoption** is becoming a practical expectation rather than just a recommendation. The Update Service allows employers to check the status of a DBS certificate at any time, rather than waiting for a full renewal. For transport providers managing large workforces, this provides ongoing assurance that staff clearances remain valid and flags any changes as they occur.

**Ongoing monitoring** is moving beyond the initial DBS check. Commissioners and regulators are placing greater emphasis on continuous assessment of staff suitability, including regular reviews of conduct, performance and any concerns that arise between formal DBS renewals. Providers who rely solely on periodic checks risk falling behind the expected standard.

**Training records and certification** are subject to increasing scrutiny during contract audits. Local authorities want to see evidence that drivers and passenger assistants have completed relevant training, including safeguarding, first aid, disability awareness and manual handling, and that refresher training is delivered on schedule. Digital training management systems make this easier to track and demonstrate.

## Fleet Emissions and Environmental Standards

Environmental compliance is rising in priority for transport operators, driven by both regulatory requirements and the expectations of commissioning bodies.

**Clean Air Zones** in cities such as Birmingham and Manchester impose charges on vehicles that do not meet specified emissions standards. For operators running large fleets, ensuring that every vehicle meets Euro 6 (or later) standards is essential to avoid significant daily charges that erode margins.

**Electric and hybrid vehicle adoption** is accelerating, although the specialist transport sector faces particular challenges. Wheelchair-accessible vehicles and ambulances have specific weight and range requirements that limit the current options for electrification. Providers are navigating a transition period, balancing the need to reduce emissions against the practical realities of their fleet requirements.

**Sustainability reporting** is becoming a factor in contract award decisions. Councils and NHS bodies are increasingly asking providers to demonstrate their environmental credentials, including fleet emissions data, recycling practices and plans for reducing their carbon footprint. Operators who can evidence a clear sustainability strategy are better positioned in competitive procurement processes.

## Digital Compliance Management

The shift from paper-based to digital compliance management is well underway, and 2026 is seeing further acceleration.

**Automated MOT and service tracking** systems alert operators to upcoming deadlines, reducing the risk of a vehicle's certification lapsing. For fleets of 200 or more vehicles, manual tracking is no longer viable. Digital systems provide dashboard visibility across the entire fleet, highlight upcoming expirations and generate audit-ready reports on demand.

**Digital vehicle inspection records** replace the paper walkaround check sheets that have been a staple of the industry for decades. Drivers complete their daily vehicle checks on a tablet or smartphone, with photos of any defects captured and timestamped automatically. This creates a more robust evidence trail and makes it easier for workshop teams to prioritise repairs.

**Integrated compliance dashboards** bring together DBS records, training certifications, MOT dates, insurance renewals and licensing information into a single view. This holistic approach to compliance management reduces the risk of items being overlooked and provides commissioners with confidence that the provider has full visibility of its obligations.

Automated alerts ensure that no deadline is missed and that every vehicle and every member of staff meets the required standards at all times.

## CQC Regulatory Developments

For providers delivering Non-Emergency Patient Transport Services, the CQC regulatory framework continues to evolve.

**Inspection methodology** is shifting towards a more data-driven approach, with the CQC using performance data, patient feedback and risk indicators to target inspections at providers where concerns are most likely. This means that providers need to maintain high standards consistently, not just during announced inspection periods.

**Duty of candour** requirements ensure that providers are transparent about incidents and near-misses. The expectation is that providers report adverse events promptly, investigate them thoroughly and share findings with patients and their families. A culture of openness around incidents is now a regulatory expectation, not just good practice.

**Workforce regulation** may see further changes, with ongoing discussions about mandatory registration for patient transport staff. While not yet implemented, providers should be prepared for a future in which their workforce is subject to individual regulatory oversight in addition to organisational CQC registration.

## Section 19 and Section 22 Permits

Community transport operators using Section 19 and Section 22 permits should be aware of continued regulatory scrutiny in this area.

The distinction between commercial and non-commercial transport services has been the subject of legal and regulatory debate, and operators must ensure that their permit usage genuinely meets the criteria set out in the Transport Act 1985. Misuse of permits can result in enforcement action and reputational damage.

## Building a Compliance Culture

The most significant trend underpinning all of these developments is the shift from compliance as a reactive, box-ticking exercise to compliance as an embedded organisational culture.

Providers who treat compliance as a strategic priority, investing in systems, training and leadership, are better placed to meet evolving requirements without disruption. Those who treat it as a cost to be minimised will find themselves increasingly exposed to regulatory risk, contract loss and reputational harm.

For councils and commissioners, the compliance culture of a transport provider is one of the most reliable indicators of overall service quality. A provider that takes its compliance obligations seriously is, almost without exception, a provider that takes its passengers seriously too.

As 2026 unfolds, the operators who thrive will be those who view compliance not as a burden, but as the framework within which excellent, safe and reliable transport is delivered every day.
